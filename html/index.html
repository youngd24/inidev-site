So, what is this?

Good question, and while the answer will likely morph over time as we tend to retcon more than even George Lucas does, the basic answer is this: as close to a faithful representation of a mid to late 90's corporate LAN/WAN we can get with a heavy bias towards 1999. Having spent much time around this era, and built multiple networks, we chose to use Cisco for the network and leverage Frame Relay for the WAN aspect. T1 circuits were a very common delivery mechansism at the time and is what the Frame Relay rides on. When possible period correct CSU/DSU devices have been used, with one or two exceptions, mainaly due to hardware being unavailble or non-working.

Why INITECH?

I suppose the basic answer is, why not? If you worked in tech in the late 90's the chances are very high that you were employed somewhere that had things like that in Office Space. Having that as the background behind this network really enables us to implement basically every stupid idea we have.

What's the backstory?

It's March of 1999 and the documentary, I mean, movie, about the company INITECH was released, you were hired soon after the fire as a contractor to help get the company back online and running smoothly again. You searchd the Internet, contacted old workmates, dug up friends, anything possible to acquire enough hardware and software to build back the bare minimum. That effort, while monumental, is mostly behind you now and you've left wondering, what's next? Out of nowhere you're pulled into a meeting with the parent company CEO, William Lumbergh Sr. and offered a job to stay on, full time, overseeing the next phase in the future of INITECH, their pivot into the next century and the world of online gaming: INIGAME.

General Guidelines and Philosophy

  • Use period correct hardware and software, earlier is fine, later is not.
  • When period correct software is not available, don't use it.
  • The one area where it is ok to deviate is in the data network provider area (BU&U).
    • In many cases we've used 2600 series routers as they were wildly popular at the time, however in order to make things work we needed additional DRAM and flash so we opted for slightly newer XM series units.
    • In the network provider area, BU&U, we were limited in the hardware available today, namely there is a lack of Cisco StrataCom gear. While we'd LOVE to build a real 1999 era Frame Relay network, we had to compromise. In order to meet space requirements we skipped the use of real CSU/DSU hardware and leverage Cisco CSU WIC cards in the Frame provider network.

VCFMW INITECH Internet Netiquette Guide
Practice Good Netiquette!

Here are some basic rules to help you be a courteous member of this community:

  • Be Respectful: You are at a Vintage Computer Festival with many, many other people that love, care for and adore old technology. They have commited to hundreds, perhaps even thousands, of hours to keeping the past alive, be respctful of that work.
  • Be mindful of your language: Avoid aggressive language, insults, or threats, and steer clear of "flaming," which is inflammatory or antagonistic criticism.
  • Don't "shout": Using all capital letters in online communication was considered rude and the equivalent of yelling.
  • Be cautious with humor and sarcasm: Without the benefit of facial expressions or tone of voice, jokes, sarcasm, and dark humor could easily be misunderstood.
  • Avoid oversharing: Be careful about sharing personal information, as it could be used for identity theft or harassment.
  • Respect privacy: Don't share someone else's private information or photos without their permission.
  • Don't spam: Sending unsolicited bulk email or overloading someone's inbox with messages was considered poor netiquette.
  • Help newcomers: Be patient and helpful to those new to online interactions.
  • Think before posting: Before sending a message or posting content, take a moment to consider whether it's something you would be comfortable saying in person.
